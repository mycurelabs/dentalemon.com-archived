# Monetizing Innovation: Knowledge Base
**Design the Product Around the Price**
*By Madhavan Ramanujam & Georg Tacke (Simon-Kucher & Partners)*

## Part 1: Core Concept

### What This Book Is About
Monetizing Innovation addresses the fundamental flaw in how most companies develop new products: they postpone pricing and monetization decisions until after the product is built, treating price as an afterthought rather than a design input. The result is a 72% failure rate for new products and services.

**The core thesis:** Companies must determine customers' willingness to pay (WTP) *before* designing and building products, then design products around validated price points—not the other way around.

### The Problem It Solves
- Products fail not because they're poorly engineered, but because companies don't know if customers will pay for them
- R&D teams build features nobody wants while missing features customers would pay for
- Pricing decisions made last-minute lead to underpriced innovations or overpriced feature bloat
- Business cases are built on hope rather than validated market data

### The Mental Model Shift

| OLD Mindset | NEW Mindset |
|-------------|-------------|
| Build product → Set price → Sell | Determine WTP → Design product → Validate price → Sell |
| Price reflects cost + margin | Price reflects customer value |
| Features = more value | Right features = value |
| One product for everyone | Segmented products for segments |
| Pricing is finance's job | Pricing is a cross-functional discipline |

## Part 3: Step-by-Step Process

### Phase 1: Discovery (Rules 1-3)

#### Step 1: Have WTP Conversations Early

**What to do:**
1. Form cross-functional internal team for expert judgment workshop
2. Develop WTP conversation guide
3. Conduct customer conversations (qualitative then quantitative)
4. Analyze WTP distribution (not just averages)

**Five WTP Research Methods (Easiest → Advanced):**

| Method | Description | Best For |
|--------|-------------|----------|
| Direct Questions | "What's an acceptable price?" / "What's expensive?" / "What's prohibitively expensive?" | Initial ballpark |
| Purchase Probability | "How likely to buy at $X?" (scale 1-5) | Quick validation |
| Most-Least | Present features, ask which most/least valued | Feature prioritization |
| Build-Your-Own | Let customer configure ideal product at price points | Configuration insights |
| Purchase Simulation (Conjoint) | Trade-off analysis across feature/price combinations | Optimal pricing |

**Key Questions to Ask:**
- What pain points does this solve?
- Which features do you value most/least? Why?
- What would you consider an acceptable price?
- At what price would it be expensive but still worth considering?
- At what price would it be too expensive to consider?
- At what price would it be so cheap you'd question quality?

**Tips:**
- Frame as "value conversation," not "pricing discussion"
- Make 25% of questions "why" questions
- Look at WTP distribution, not just averages
- Conduct qualitative before quantitative
- Include all customer types, not just friendly ones

#### Step 2: Segment by WTP

**Golden Rule:** Segment so you can ACT DIFFERENTLY for each segment.

**Segment on:**
- Customer needs
- Value perception
- Willingness to pay

**Do NOT segment primarily on:**
- Demographics (age, gender)
- Firmographics (company size, industry)
- Observable characteristics alone

**Segmentation Principles:**
1. Begin with WTP data
2. Let common sense guide statistics
3. Fewer segments is more (start with 3-4)
4. Don't try to serve every segment
5. Describe segments with observable criteria for targeting

**Example Segments (Paper Company):**
| Segment | Priority | WTP Profile |
|---------|----------|-------------|
| Want Price Only | Low price | Low—basic needs only |
| Want It Now | Fast delivery | Medium—pays for speed |
| Want Product Only | Quality | Medium-High—pays for performance |
| Want the Best | Full service | High—premium for quality + service |

#### Step 3: Configure Products & Bundle

**Product Configuration:** Which features go into which product for which segment

**Bundling:** Whether to combine products/services together

**Classify Features:**

| Type | Definition | Action |
|------|------------|--------|
| **Leaders** | Must-haves, high WTP, drive purchase | Include in core |
| **Fillers** | Nice-to-haves, moderate WTP | Include selectively |
| **Killers** | Features <20% value, >20% reject | Exclude or sell à la carte |

**Killer Feature Test:** Valued by <20% of customers AND rejected by >20%

**Good/Better/Best (G/B/B) Structure:**

| Tier | Target | Ideal Share |
|------|--------|-------------|
| Good | Price-conscious | ≤25-30% |
| Better | Balance seekers | 40-50% |
| Best | Quality-conscious | ≥10-20% |

**Configuration Tips:**
1. Align with segments
2. Keep bundles under 9 benefits / 4 products
3. Both you AND customer must benefit
4. Don't overload entry-level (or you can't upsell)
5. Hard bundling only works with market power
6. Individual prices must be higher in mixed bundles
7. Communicate bundle value strategically
8. Bundle for integration value (1+1=3)
9. Don't bundle unnecessarily
10. Exploit inverse correlations across segments

### Phase 2: Architecture (Rules 4-6)

#### Step 4: Choose Monetization Model

**Core Insight:** HOW you charge often matters more than HOW MUCH you charge.

**Five Proven Models:**

| Model | Description | Best When |
|-------|-------------|-----------|
| **Subscription** | Recurring periodic payment | Continuous use; high churn risk; want predictable revenue |
| **Dynamic Pricing** | Price varies by demand/time/customer | Demand fluctuates; constrained capacity; price elasticity varies |
| **Auction** | Market-determined pricing via bidding | Seller's market; unique inventory; strong demand |
| **Alternative Metric** | Price on value metric, not units | Value tied to usage/outcomes; traditional metrics don't capture value |
| **Freemium** | Free basic tier, paid premium | Near-zero marginal cost; clear premium value; high conversion potential |

**Five Questions to Select Model:**
1. Will customers accept this model?
2. How will future trends affect this model?
3. Does it fit your company's stage and position?
4. What's your competitive situation?
5. Do you have capabilities to execute it?

**Price Structures:**
- **Tier/Volume-Based:** Better rates at higher volumes
- **Matrix Model:** Two-dimensional tiers (e.g., volume × wallet share)

#### Step 5: Develop Pricing Strategy

**Definition:** Your short- and long-term monetization plan with clear intent, quantifiable goals, and time frame.

**Building Block 1: Set Clear Goals**

Use Goal Allocation Exercise: Distribute 100 points across goals to force trade-offs.

Possible goals: Revenue | Profit | Market share | Margin | Customer lifetime value | ARPU

**Building Block 2: Pick Strategy Type**

| Strategy | Description | When to Use |
|----------|-------------|-------------|
| **Maximization** | Optimize for current period | No early adopters with higher WTP; stable market |
| **Penetration** | Price low to gain share fast | Network effects; high loyalty to first choice; future upsell opportunity |
| **Skimming** | Start high, lower over time | Significant early adopter segment; breakthrough product; capacity constraints |

**Building Block 3: Price-Setting Principles**
- Monetization model by segment/lifecycle
- Price differentiation factors (channel, region, vertical)
- Price floors and maximum discounts
- Price endings (0, .50, .99, .95)
- Price increase frequency and magnitude

**Building Block 4: Reaction Principles**
- Promotional principles (when, how, to whom)
- Competitive response principles (war-game scenarios)

#### Step 6: Build Outside-In Business Case

**Four Pillars (must be linked):**

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                                         │
│    VALUE ←→ PRICE ←→ VOLUME ←→ COST    │
│                                         │
└─────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Critical:** Changes to one pillar must flow through to others.

**Nine Steps to Build Business Case:**
1. Forget static business cases—make it living
2. Assemble ingredients: market size, segments, offers, WTP, costs, competitors
3. Include price elasticity
4. Use data-verified facts (avoid inflated assumptions)
5. Add risk assumptions and scenarios
6. Be realistic about goal trade-offs
7. Model competitive reactions
8. Assess impact on existing products (cannibalization)
9. Keep checking in at each milestone

### Phase 3: Execution (Rules 7-9)

#### Step 7: Communicate Value

**Core Problem:** Marketing/sales teams are often disconnected from innovation teams and come in too late.

**Three Steps:**

**Step 7.1: Develop Benefit Statements (Not Feature Descriptions)**
- Feature = belongs to product
- Benefit = belongs to customer
- Ask: "What does customer achieve because of this feature?"

**Step 7.2: Make Messages Segment-Specific**
- Different segments need different value messages
- Tailor to each segment's priorities

**Step 7.3: Measure Impact and Refine**
- Track customer perception of communicated value
- Use MOCA analysis regularly

**Matrix of Competitive Advantages (MOCA):**

```
                    High Importance to Customer
                              │
        Competitive           │         Competitive
        Disadvantages         │         Advantages
        (Prepare defenses)    │         (EMPHASIZE)
                              │
    ──────────────────────────┼──────────────────────────
                              │
        Low Priority          │         Latent Strengths
        (De-emphasize)        │         (Convince customers
                              │          these matter)
                              │
                    Low Importance to Customer

    Worse vs Competition ←────┼────→ Better vs Competition
```

#### Step 8: Use Behavioral Pricing Tactics

**Premise:** Customers make decisions based on both rational AND irrational factors.

**Six Powerful Tactics:**

| Tactic | Description | Application |
|--------|-------------|-------------|
| **Compromise Effect** | People avoid extremes | Offer Good/Better/Best; steer to middle |
| **Anchoring** | Reference points shape perception | Lead with high anchor price; use premium tier as anchor |
| **Price = Quality Signal** | High price signals high quality | Don't underprice; price reflects value |
| **Razor/Razor Blades** | Low entry, high consumables | Land cheap, expand with follow-on purchases |
| **Pennies-a-Day** | Break down to small units | $9.99/month vs. $120/year |
| **Psychological Thresholds** | Price cliffs exist | Stay at $99, not $101; identify your thresholds |

**Test Behavioral Tactics Via:**
1. Focus groups (watch reactions)
2. A/B tests (online)
3. Large-scale survey experiments

#### Step 9: Maintain Price Integrity

**Definition:** Resist knee-jerk price cuts when initial sales disappoint.

**Why Price Integrity Matters:**
- Erodes profit margin permanently
- Damages brand perception
- Reduces customer lifetime value
- Signals lack of confidence

**Post-Launch Discipline:**

| Problem | Response |
|---------|----------|
| Sales below plan | Form task force; diagnose root cause BEFORE considering price |
| Competition undercuts | War-game responses; find non-price actions |
| Competition launches rival | Assess value positioning; don't panic |
| Sales above plan | Investigate—you may have underpriced (minivation) |

**Track These KPIs:**
- Win/loss ratio and reasons
- Price deviation from target
- Deal escalation rate (healthy = 20-30%)
- Price changes upon escalation
- Feature usage by segment

**The 3-Nonprice-Actions Rule:**
Before approving any price cut, require team to propose three non-price alternatives first.

## Part 4: Supporting Concepts

### The Four Types of Monetization Failure

| Type | Definition | Cause | Warning Signs |
|------|------------|-------|---------------|
| **Feature Shock** | Too many features, overpriced, doesn't resonate | Engineering/product-driven culture; "let's add this" | R&D can't articulate value; one-size-fits-all; tepid customer response |
| **Minivation** | Right product, wrong (too low) price | Risk-averse culture; "good enough" mindset | Easy sales; immediate profit pressure acceptance; "what might have been" |
| **Hidden Gem** | Brilliant idea never brought to market or given away | Coddling core business; can't see value | Excitement about concept; given away as deal sweetener; killed by middle management |
| **Undead** | Product nobody wants, brought to market anyway | Top-down culture; can't say no; yes-maybe-no culture | Delusional attachment; sales can't sell; customers intrigued but won't buy |

### Company Culture → Failure Type Mapping

| Culture | Likely Failure |
|---------|----------------|
| Strong engineering/product-driven | Feature Shock |
| Risk-averse, play-it-safe | Minivation |
| Core-business-protective | Hidden Gem |
| Top-down, discourages feedback | Undead |

### Price Elasticity Fundamentals

**Definition:** How much sales volume changes when price changes

```
Price Elasticity = % Change in Volume / % Change in Price
```

**Key Insights:**
- Every product has an elasticity curve—find yours
- Steeper curve = more price-sensitive customers
- Lower margins typically mean higher optimal elasticity
- Industry averages are useless—measure YOUR product
- Use elasticity to model price-volume-profit trade-offs

### Value Pricing vs. Behavioral Pricing

| Value Pricing | Behavioral Pricing |
|---------------|-------------------|
| Rational | Irrational |
| Based on customer value perception | Based on psychological factors |
| Matches price to value delivered | Frames price for easier decisions |
| Foundation | Refinement |

**Combine both for optimal results.**

## Part 5: Common Traps & Mistakes

### Strategic Mistakes

1. **Waiting until end to price** — Treating price as last step
2. **Cost-plus pricing** — Ignoring customer value
3. **One-size-fits-all** — Not segmenting by WTP
4. **Feature bloat** — Adding features without WTP validation
5. **Underpricing** — Leaving money on table
6. **Inside-out thinking** — Building what you find cool vs. what customers pay for

### Segmentation Mistakes

1. **Segmenting too late** — After product is designed
2. **Segmenting by demographics only** — Prince Charles ≠ Ozzy Osbourne
3. **Too many segmentation schemes** — Different ones for marketing, sales, product
4. **Averaging across segments** — Hiding real opportunities

### Business Case Mistakes

1. **Static documents** — Created once for funding, then forgotten
2. **Missing WTP data** — Guessing at price acceptance
3. **No price elasticity** — Can't model trade-offs
4. **Unverified assumptions** — Overstated market size, adoption rates
5. **Ignoring cannibalization** — Not modeling impact on existing products
6. **No competitive reaction modeling** — Assuming rivals do nothing

### Post-Launch Mistakes

1. **Knee-jerk price cuts** — First response to slow sales
2. **Only tracking financial KPIs** — Missing operational/sales insights
3. **No deal deconstructions** — Not learning from wins/losses
4. **Starting price wars** — Initiating destructive competition
5. **Ignoring high sales** — Not investigating potential underpricing

### Implementation Mistakes

1. **Putting all eggs in one basket** — Depending on one person/group
2. **No cross-functional team** — R&D isolated from marketing/sales
3. **Big bang approach** — Trying to change everything at once
4. **One-size-fits-all process** — Not adapting to company context
5. **Too many opt-outs** — Allowing reversion to old ways
6. **Getting blinded by science** — Over-optimizing before designing
7. **Avoiding messy information** — Expecting perfect data
8. **Cheaping out** — Insufficient budget for research
9. **C-suite delegation** — Leaders not personally involved

## Part 6: Implementation

### Two-Phase Approach

```
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  JUMP-START & PILOT          │  SCALE & STICK            │
│  "What can we start           │  "How do we               │
│   doing tomorrow?"            │   institutionalize?"      │
├───────────────────────────────┼───────────────────────────┤
│  • Diagnose current process   │  • Document process       │
│  • Select pilot product       │  • Build monetization     │
│  • Form cross-functional team │    team                   │
│  • Appoint monetization hero  │  • Assign roles/          │
│  • Conduct WTP research       │    responsibilities       │
│  • Apply nine rules to pilot  │  • Track KPIs             │
│  • Document learnings         │  • Review and iterate     │
│                               │  • Train organization     │
│                               │  • Change culture         │
└───────────────────────────────┴───────────────────────────┘
```

### Jump-Start Steps

1. **Diagnose:** Select recent products, compare actual vs. planned performance
2. **Identify gaps:** Which of nine rules were missing?
3. **Participate:** Attend early R&D meetings; challenge teams
4. **Educate:** Explain vision and nine rules

### Pilot Steps

1. Select representative product in pipeline
2. Form cross-functional team (R&D, product, pricing, marketing, sales, finance)
3. Appoint monetization hero (accountable for initiative)
4. Establish rules of engagement:
   - Hold each other accountable
   - Empower people to say "no"
5. Allocate budget for WTP research
6. Apply all nine rules; document what works

### Scale Steps (Multi-Product Companies)

1. Select one product from each major product group
2. Create/update process workflows and playbooks
3. Dedicate monetization team (can be same as innovation team in startups)
4. Assign clear roles using RACI model
5. Establish regular cross-functional meetings with CEO questions
6. Track comprehensive KPIs (financial + operational + customer)
7. Implement review schedule

### Stick Steps

1. Design training plan for skill gaps
2. Create change management plan for each function
3. Lead by example (C-suite involvement)
4. Tell success stories from pilots
5. Learn to say "no" to unworthy ideas
6. Create "no/maybe/yes" culture (opposite of "yes/maybe/no")

### Organizational Requirements

**Cross-Functional Team Roles (RACI):**

| Rule | Product | Marketing | Sales | Finance | R&D |
|------|---------|-----------|-------|---------|-----|
| WTP Talks | R | C | C | I | R |
| Segmentation | R | A | C | I | C |
| Configuration | R | C | C | I | A |
| Monetization Model | R | R | C | A | C |
| Pricing Strategy | R | R | C | A | C |
| Business Case | R | C | C | A | C |
| Value Comms | C | A | R | I | C |
| Behavioral Pricing | R | A | R | C | I |
| Price Integrity | C | R | A | R | I |

*R = Responsible, A = Accountable, C = Consulted, I = Informed*

## Part 7: Templates & Checklists

### WTP Conversation Guide

**Opening:**
"We'd like to talk about our latest innovation ideas and how we can continue to add value for you."

**Phase 1: Pain Points**
- What challenges do you face with [problem area]?
- How do you currently address this?
- What's not working well?

**Phase 2: Value Exploration**
- [Describe concept/features]
- Which of these would be most valuable? Why?
- Which wouldn't matter much? Why?

**Phase 3: WTP Questions**
- What would be an acceptable price for this?
- At what price would it be expensive but still worth considering?
- At what price would it be too expensive regardless of value?
- At what price would it be so cheap you'd question quality?

### Segmentation Validation Checklist

- [ ] Segments based on needs, value, and WTP (not just demographics)
- [ ] Can articulate how to act differently for each segment
- [ ] Clear "fences" exist between segments
- [ ] Sales team confirms they can identify customers in each segment
- [ ] Each segment is large enough to justify serving
- [ ] 3-4 segments maximum to start

### Feature Classification Worksheet

For each feature, estimate:

| Feature | % Value Highly | % Value Somewhat | % No Value | Classification |
|---------|---------------|------------------|------------|----------------|
| [Name] | ___% | ___% | ___% | L / F / K |

- **Leader:** High % value highly
- **Filler:** Moderate across
- **Killer:** <20% value + >20% no value

### CEO Questions by Chapter

**Ch. 4 - WTP Talks:**
1. Did we have WTP conversations? When?
2. What data shows viable market will pay?
3. Do we know acceptable/expensive price ranges?
4. Do we know which features customers will/won't pay for?
5. What differentiates us vs. competitors in customer eyes?

**Ch. 5 - Segmentation:**
1. Did we segment before designing?
2. What are segments and how did we identify them?
3. How different is WTP across segments?
4. Can we describe segments with observable criteria?
5. How many segmentation schemes exist? Can we consolidate?

**Ch. 6 - Configuration:**
1. What configurations/bundles will we offer? Why?
2. What are leader/filler/killer features?
3. Did we explore G/B/B? What's expected share per tier?
4. Did we explore bundling with existing products?
5. Did we consider unbundling?

**Ch. 7 - Monetization Model:**
1. What model did we choose? Why?
2. Which models did we reject? Why?
3. How do industry trends affect model choice?
4. How will we handle variable usage?
5. Do we have capabilities to execute?

**Ch. 8 - Pricing Strategy:**
1. Is there a pricing strategy document?
2. What are priority goals? Is team aligned?
3. Which strategy type? Why?
4. What's the price elasticity curve?
5. Are price-setting principles clear?
6. Do we have reaction plans?

**Ch. 9 - Business Case:**
1. Does business case include WTP data?
2. Are four pillars modeled with linkages?
3. Is it a living document?
4. Can we simulate scenarios?
5. Are competitive responses anticipated?

**Ch. 10 - Value Communications:**
1. What benefits do customers derive?
2. Is marketing/sales involved early?
3. Are value messages segment-specific and tested?
4. Did entire innovation team approve materials?
5. How will we measure message effectiveness?

**Ch. 11 - Behavioral Pricing:**
1. Did we consider behavioral tactics?
2. Which of six tactics apply?
3. Do we have anchor products? Know psychological thresholds?
4. How did we test effectiveness?

**Ch. 12 - Price Integrity:**
1. Do we have post-launch monitoring plan?
2. What problems might arise? What's our response?
3. What non-price actions will we try before cutting price?
4. How do we ensure price cuts are last resort?
5. How will we analyze higher-than-expected sales?

## Part 8: How to Use This Document

### For New Product Development

1. **Discovery Phase:** Start with WTP research using conversation guide
2. **Segmentation:** Use checklist to validate segments
3. **Configuration:** Classify features, design G/B/B
4. **Strategy:** Complete pricing strategy template
5. **Business Case:** Use checklist to ensure completeness
6. **Launch Prep:** Develop value communications using MOCA
7. **Post-Launch:** Track KPIs, maintain price integrity

### For Diagnosing Existing Products

1. Identify which failure type applies (feature shock/minivation/hidden gem/undead)
2. Review which of nine rules were missing
3. Determine if repositioning, repricing, or retirement is warranted

### For Organizational Transformation

1. Run diagnostic on recent product launches
2. Select pilot product
3. Form cross-functional team with monetization hero
4. Apply nine rules to pilot
5. Document and scale
